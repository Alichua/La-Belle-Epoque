# Newspaper Layout Research Notes

Use these as layout heuristics and scan-search guardrails. Prefer actual historical scan/page-image references over modern newspaper websites. The skill uses five real masthead assets per country by default, randomly selected and recolored into the template's paper/ink colors; fictional mastheads are fallback assets only.

## Reference Search Rules

- Search for `front page scan`, `page image`, `newspaper archive`, `microfilm`, `digitized newspaper`, `IIIF`, `masthead crop`, and exact years inside the country's Belle Epoque period.
- Treat current newspaper homepages, article pages, and brand/marketing pages as contaminated layout references unless the target era itself is the web era and the user explicitly wants web influence.
- Prefer scan-bearing archives and image records: Gallica/BnF, LOC/Chronicling America, national libraries, university archives, HKPL/SCMP archive descriptions, Wikimedia Commons public-domain scans, and similar digitized collections.
- For final assets, separate `layout reference` from `embedded asset`: page scans inform column rhythm, masthead scale, ad density, and rule placement; local `real-mastheads/` assets provide the actual masthead image.

## France

Reference anchors:

- RetroNews/BnF describes `Le Petit Journal` as a mass popular daily with industrial production, low price, half-format pages, larger type, and more airy layouts for readability.
- Its illustrated supplement relied on bold front/back cover imagery, which maps well to a large lead image plus ornate masthead/rules.
- A public-domain `Le Petit Journal` header image is available on Wikimedia Commons and is processed as a local real masthead asset.

Layout translation:

- Tall decorative masthead.
- Cream paper, black ink, red or teal accent rules.
- Large lead image with a caption band.
- Narrow lower columns and illustrated ad cards.
- Art Nouveau corner flourishes and poster-like ad panels.

## United States

Reference anchors:

- `USA Today`, launched in 1982, is a key 1980s reference for color-coded sections, graphic front-page treatment, and modular entry points.
- Late-20th-century American front pages increasingly used photography and clearer modular hierarchy.
- USA Today Network's current print description still frames the paper around colorful News, Money, Sports, and Life sections; this is useful as a simplified visual grammar, not a historical logo source.

Layout translation:

- Bold masthead bar and color-coded section chips.
- Strong boxes, ticker strip, weather/market mini-panels.
- Cleaner sans-serif headline rhythm rendered through the shared pixel font.
- Ad cards for computers, malls, VHS, sneakers, cars, diners, and finance.

## Japan

Reference anchors:

- Japanese newspaper design historically kept dense vertical column rhythm; official Japanese layout guidance notes that major newspapers are largely set in vertical writing mode, with images, tables, captions, and some headings placed horizontally.
- The Japan Newspaper Publishers & Editors Association noted a later trend toward horizontal text and columns, especially where tabloid-size layouts and finance/investment pages benefited from horizontal flow.
- Showa/bubble-era design should mix orderly newspaper grids with city-pop color tabs and small vertical glyph texture.

Layout translation:

- Compact evening-paper grid.
- Masthead with clean rule boxes and small accent tabs.
- Abstract vertical glyph columns as texture; readable English text still uses the shared pixel font.
- Ad cards for cassette tapes, record shops, taxis, vending machines, cameras, department-store bags, train tickets, and karaoke.

## China

Reference anchors:

- `People's Daily` represents regimented party-paper structure; reform-era media expanded rapidly through the 1980s and 1990s.
- Chinese tabloid formats became extremely popular in the mid-1990s after subsidy changes, bringing bolder formats and more commercial pressure.
- Studies of post-Mao tabloid journalism describe the 1990s metro-paper boom as reader-oriented, market-driven, local-news heavy, and more sensational in writing and layout.

Layout translation:

- Sturdy title rule, red civic accent, gridded announcement feel.
- Use practical columns and boxed notices, with optional market-oriented ad rail.
- Ad cards for appliances, bicycles, cranes, real-estate boards, pager/mobile shops, factory gates, market signs, and bus tickets.

## Hong Kong

Reference anchors:

- Historical SCMP archives cover 1903-1995 and include page views, advertisements, cartoons, and photographs.
- `Apple Daily`, launched in 1995, introduced Hong Kong tabloid journalism; it was modelled after `USA Today`, printed in full color, used concise writing, large colorful graphics, and pushed tabloid-style visual emphasis.

Layout translation:

- Dense commercial front page with neon ad strip.
- Big lead image, compressed boxes, finance/weather ticker, wet-ink contrast.
- Ad cards for taxi panels, watch shops, tea restaurants, ferry tickets, cinema posters, electronics signs, minibus placards, and jewelry.

## Korea

Reference anchors:

- A long-run study of `Chosun Ilbo` and `Dong-A Ilbo` front pages divides change into 1921-1930, 1931-1990, and 1991-2001, with front pages shifting from opinion-centered makeup toward straight-news design and more visualization.
- Korean newspaper photo/front-page studies point to 1980-1990 as a turning point for stronger visualization, captions, and more independent photo communication.
- Library of Congress notes `The Korea Times` as Seoul-based and Korea's first English daily, useful for an English masthead fallback.

Layout translation:

- Clean developmental daily with orderly columns.
- Sport/civic sidebars, Olympic color dots, bank/electronics ad blocks.
- Ad cards for sedans, Olympic pennants, TV sets, bank windows, department-store bags, school notebooks, apartment keys, and coffee.
